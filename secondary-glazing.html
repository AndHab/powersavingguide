Secondary glazing: warmth without new windows

Secondary glazing adds a second layer of glazing on the room side of an existing window, trapping a pocket of still air that does the insulating. It will never quite match a sealed double-glazed unit, but it costs a fraction as much, keeps your original windows, and gets you most of the way there. For listed buildings, conservation areas, rented homes and anyone who cannot justify the price of new windows, it is the most sensible way to warm up a cold single-glazed room. This guide covers how it works, the options from cheap film to fitted panels, what it saves, and when it is the right call.

The short answer. Secondary glazing fits an inner pane a few centimetres behind your existing window, trapping still air that slows heat loss, kills the chilly downdraught and cuts condensation and outside noise. Options run from shrink-film at a few pounds per window to magnetic acrylic panels and permanent fitted aluminium units at a few hundred pounds each. It saves less than full double glazing but costs far less, suits period and listed homes, and pays back faster on the cheaper options.

How a trapped pocket of air keeps you warm

The enemy at a window is the cold pane and the air moving around it. Heat radiates out through single glazing, and the cold glass chills the air next to it, which sinks and slides across the floor as that familiar draught past your ankles. A single sheet of glass is a poor insulator.

Add an inner layer a few centimetres back and you create a buffer of still, trapped air between the two panes. Still air is a good insulator, so it slows the heat escaping. The inner pane stays close to room temperature, so the downdraught largely stops, the room feels warmer at the same thermostat setting, and condensation no longer forms on the cold original glass because the warm room air no longer touches it.

As a bonus, that air gap and second pane noticeably dampen outside noise, which is why secondary glazing is popular near busy roads, flight paths and railways quite apart from any energy benefit. The gap matters: a wider air gap, around 100mm to 200mm, insulates and soundproofs better than a narrow one, which is one way secondary glazing can actually beat slim double-glazed units on noise.

The options, from a few pounds to a proper fit

Secondary glazing covers a wide range, from a temporary winter fix to a permanent fitting. The right one depends on budget, how it needs to look, and whether you rent or own.

At the cheap end, shrink-fit film is double-sided tape around the frame, a sheet of film, and a hairdryer to warm it tight and crystal-clear. It costs very little and works surprisingly well for a winter, though it is not pretty and goes on and off seasonally. Magnetic or clip-in acrylic panels are a tidy middle ground you can cut and make yourself, popping off for cleaning or summer. At the top end, slim aluminium-framed units are fitted permanently inside the reveal and slide or hinge open like a proper window, which is what you want for a room you use every day or a noise problem.

What it saves a year

The saving depends on how much glass you treat and how cold the room was to begin with. Single glazing loses a lot of heat, and an unheated or barely heated cold room is where secondary glazing earns most. As a rough guide, secondary glazing typically cuts heat loss through a window by around half to two thirds, less than the roughly three-quarters a good sealed double-glazed unit manages, but at a tiny fraction of the cost.

Made for period and listed homes

Secondary glazing comes into its own where replacement is impossible or unwanted. Listed buildings and conservation areas often forbid swapping original windows, and owners of period houses frequently want to keep handsome old sashes rather than rip them out for plastic.

Because secondary glazing sits discreetly behind the existing window and changes nothing outside, it sidesteps planning and listed-building objections while still warming the room. The original window stays exactly as it was, and the inner layer is barely visible from inside and invisible from the street. For a draughty Georgian or Victorian house it is often the only sensible way to cut window heat loss at all.

It is also the natural choice for renters. A landlord will rarely pay for new windows, but removable film or clip-in panels need no permission, cause no damage and come away cleanly at the end of the tenancy. Our saving energy when renting guide leans on exactly this kind of reversible fix.

What it will not do, and the mistakes to avoid

Secondary glazing is good value, but set expectations honestly so you are not disappointed.

It will not turn a single-glazed window into a modern double-glazed one. You still have the original single pane doing part of the work, so the overall performance lands below a sealed double-glazed unit. It also does nothing for heat lost through the wall, the floor or the loft, which in many homes lose far more than the windows do, so it is one piece of a bigger job rather than a fix on its own.

The common mistakes are easy to avoid once you know them. A gappy or loose inner layer leaks the trapped air and throws away most of the benefit, so the seal is everything. Cheap film left up year-round yellows and looks shabby, so treat it as a winter measure. And condensation can form between the panes if moist room air gets into the cavity through a poor seal, which looks alarming but is cured by sealing the inner layer properly and giving the room some ventilation. Finally, do not block trickle vents or fixed background ventilation, since a house sealed too tightly traps moisture and grows mould, the same trap covered in our draught-proofing guide.

Seal it well, and pair it with the cheap stuff

The trapped air only insulates if it stays trapped. A good seal around the secondary layer matters more than the glazing material itself. A loose film, a gappy panel or a unit that does not close properly lets warm room air leak into the cavity and the benefit drains away. Take care with the edges, and on permanent units make sure the seals are sound.

Secondary glazing also works best as part of a cluster of cheap fixes rather than alone. Draught-proofing the main window stops cold air sneaking in around the frame, which secondary glazing on its own does not fully fix. Heavy lined curtains closed at dusk add another insulating layer over the glass at night, and a radiator reflector behind a radiator under the window pushes heat back into the room. Together, a cold single-glazed room becomes genuinely comfortable for very little outlay.
