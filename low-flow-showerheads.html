Low-flow showerheads: save water and the energy to heat it

A shower is one of the larger hot-water draws in most homes, and the bulk of what it costs is the energy used to heat the water, not the water itself. Cut the litres per minute and you cut both at once. A low-flow or aerating showerhead does that for the price of a takeaway, often without the feeble dribble people fear. The catch is that it works brilliantly on some plumbing and does nothing useful, or even causes trouble, on others. This guide works through the saving at current UK prices and tells you which camp you are in before you spend a penny.

The short answer. A low-flow or aerating showerhead drops a typical mixer shower from around 12 litres a minute to 7 or 8, cutting the hot water you use by a third to a half. For a household showering daily that can save £40 to £100 a year in heating energy, plus water charges if you are metered, for a head costing £10 to £25. It suits mains-pressure and combi-fed mixer showers. Leave electric showers and weak gravity-fed systems alone.

Why the saving is mostly energy, not water

When you stand under a shower you are paying for two things at once. The water, which only counts if you are on a meter, and the energy that heated it, which counts for everyone with a gas boiler, immersion or electric shower. The energy is by far the bigger number.

Heating water is expensive because it takes a lot of energy to raise its temperature. Warming the water for a single eight minute mixer shower can use roughly 2 to 3 kWh. At the gas rate of 7.33p per kWh that is around 15p to 22p of gas a shower, and far more if you heat with electricity. Spread across a family showering every day, the hot water for showers is one of the biggest single uses in the house, second only to the heating itself. Our hot water savings guide covers the full picture.

This is why the showerhead matters out of all proportion to its price. Every litre of hot water you do not draw is a litre you did not pay to heat. Trim the flow and the saving lands on the energy bill first, the water bill second.

It also explains why showers usually cost far more to run than baths get blamed for. A long power shower can draw more hot water than a bath, so the cheap showerhead is doing real work every single day, not just shaving pennies off an occasional treat.

How a low-flow head keeps the feel without the flow

People resist low-flow heads because they imagine a sad trickle. Good ones avoid that in one of two ways.

An aerating head sucks air into the water stream, so the spray feels full and soft while actually pushing fewer litres through. The drops are bulked out with air. A laminar or restricted-flow head uses cleverly shaped nozzles to keep the pressure and the punch up while narrowing the openings. Either way the aim is a satisfying shower from fewer litres a minute.

A standard mixer showerhead often runs at 10 to 15 litres a minute. A decent low-flow head brings that down to 6 to 8 while still feeling like a proper shower. For most people the difference in feel, once fitted, is barely noticeable. Aerating heads cool slightly faster because the air chills the droplets, so you may nudge the temperature up a touch, but the net hot-water saving still stands.

What it actually saves a year

Here is a worked example for a household of four, each taking one eight minute shower a day, heated by a gas combi. The figures use the flow rate before and after fitting a low-flow head.

So fitting an 8 L/min head saves this household around £100 a year on gas alone, and a 6 L/min aerating head closer to £160. Add the water and sewerage charges on top if you are metered, which can roughly double the headline saving. Against a head costing £10 to £25, that pays back in weeks. Heat your water with electricity at 26.11p per kWh, as an electric shower or immersion heater does, and the saving per litre is over three times larger again.

Which showers it suits, and which to leave alone

This is the part that gets skipped, and it is the part that matters most. A low-flow head is right for some showers and pointless or harmful on others.

  • Mains-pressure and combi-fed mixer showers. The sweet spot. Plenty of pressure, a fixed plumbed head, and a clear saving from restricting the flow. Fit away.
  • Electric showers. Leave them. An electric shower heats water on demand and already governs its own flow to match the power of its element. Bolting a flow restrictor on the outlet can make the unit overheat and trip its safety cut-out, and it saves nothing because the shower was already limiting flow. The way to cut an electric shower's cost is a shorter shower or a lower power setting, not the head.
  • Low-pressure gravity-fed systems. Older homes with a cold tank in the loft and a hot cylinder may already have weak pressure. Restrict the flow further and you get a genuine dribble. Some low-flow heads are designed for low pressure, so check the spec, but a standard restrictor will disappoint here.

If you are not sure what you have, look at the shower itself. A boxy unit on the wall with its own power switch is electric. A simple valve fed from the household hot water is a mixer. Knowing which you have saves a wasted purchase.

Cheaper and free options first

Before buying anything, two things cost nothing.

The first is the habit. A four minute shower uses half the water of an eight minute one, whatever head you have. A cheap shower timer or just a favourite four minute song on the bathroom speaker nudges the habit without any kit. Cutting the time is the single biggest lever and it is free.

The second is your water company. Many UK water companies give away aerators, flow regulators and even whole low-flow showerheads free through their water-efficiency schemes, along with tap inserts and cistern bags. It is worth a two minute search of your supplier's site before you pay for one. The same schemes often post them out at no charge.

If you rent, a clip-on or screw-on low-flow head is a tidy, reversible swap you can take with you when you leave, which is the kind of fix our saving energy when renting guide favours. Keep the original head in a drawer and refit it at the end of the tenancy.

Where it fits in the bigger hot-water picture

A showerhead is one piece of a cheap, high-return cluster of hot-water fixes. None of them is dramatic on its own, but together they take a real bite out of the bill.

Lag the pipes and jacket the cylinder so the heat you pay for does not leak away before it reaches the tap, as covered in cylinder jacket and pipe lagging. If you have a stored-cylinder system, check the thermostat is set sensibly rather than scalding. And if you want to see exactly where your own hot water goes, a plug-in energy monitor on an electric shower or immersion tells you the truth for your house.
