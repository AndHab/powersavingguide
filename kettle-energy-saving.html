Boiling the kettle for less

A kettle is one of the most powerful appliances in an ordinary home, pulling around 3kW, more than most electric heaters. Each boil is brief, so no single one frightens the bill, but a tea-drinking household boils it many times a day, year after year. That makes the kettle a worthwhile target, and unlike most savings this one costs nothing to act on. The waste is almost entirely in overfilling, and the fix is simply boiling what you need. This guide puts real pence on each boil and runs through the habits that trim them.

The short answer. A 3kW kettle costs about 2.6p to boil a single mug and around 8p to boil a full litre and a half, at 26.11p per kWh. Boil it five times a day filled to the top when you only need one mug and you waste roughly £40 a year heating water you tip away. Boiling only what you need is the single biggest saving and it is free. Descaling keeps the kettle quick, and the kettle itself is already efficient, so the waste is in how you use it, not the appliance.

What a boil actually costs

A kettle's power is high but its run time is short, which is why the cost per boil surprises people in both directions. It is small per boil, but it stacks up fast.

A 3kW kettle uses 3 kWh for every hour it runs, so a boil that takes one minute uses about 0.05 kWh. Bringing water from cold to the boil depends mostly on how much water is in there. Here is the cost at 26.11p per kWh.

The lesson is in the gap between the rows. Boil a full kettle five times a day when you only wanted a mug each time, and you spend about £80 a year. Boil just the mug and it is £12. That £68 difference is pure waste, heating water you pour straight down the sink. To see your own kitchen's numbers, put your habits through the appliance running cost calculator.

Only boil what you need

This is the whole game. The single biggest kettle saving is boiling only the water you are about to use, and it costs nothing but a moment's attention.

The easy method is to measure your mug into the kettle rather than filling from the tap by eye. Fill the mug with cold water, tip it in, and you have boiled exactly one cup's worth. Most kettles also have a window gauge with cup markings, so fill to the line for the number of drinks you are making. Either way you stop heating three spare cups every single brew.

There is a floor to this. Every kettle has a minimum fill, usually marked, that keeps the element covered. Boiling below that risks the element running dry and cutting out, so do not go under the minimum line. But the gap between that minimum and a full kettle is where almost all the waste lives.

If overfilling is a stubborn habit, it is one of the cheapest behaviours to fix on the whole quick wins under a tenner list, because it costs nothing at all.

Descale for speed and efficiency

In a hard-water area, limescale builds up on the element and the base. That chalky crust gets between the element and the water, so heat has to fight through it. The kettle takes longer to boil and uses more energy doing it, and a badly scaled kettle is both slower and thirstier.

Descaling is cheap and quick. A dedicated kettle descaler works, but ordinary white vinegar or a sachet of citric acid does the job for pennies. Fill the kettle with a half-and-half mix of water and vinegar, or a couple of spoons of citric acid in water, boil it, leave it to sit for an hour, then rinse thoroughly two or three times so no taste lingers. Do it every month or two in a hard-water area, less often where the water is soft.

It will not transform your bill on its own, but a clear element boils faster, which means less energy per boil and a kettle that lasts longer before the element burns out. Free habit, small saving, longer-lived kettle.

Eco kettles, rapid boil and the flask trick

The shops sell several kettle features as energy savers. Most are convenience dressed up as efficiency.

  • Eco kettles with clear gauges and low minimum fills mostly just make it easier to boil less, which you can already do for free by paying attention. The kettle is not more efficient, it just encourages a better habit. Worth it if it actually changes your behaviour, not worth a premium if it does not.
  • Rapid-boil kettles save time, not energy. A more powerful element boils the same water faster, using roughly the same total energy. Nice if you are impatient, neutral on the bill.
  • Variable-temperature kettles let you stop at 80C for green tea or coffee instead of a full boil. Heating to 80C rather than 100C genuinely uses a little less energy, so this one has a small real saving if you drink a lot of drinks that do not need boiling water.

The genuinely useful trick costs nothing extra if you own a flask. If you drink several hot drinks across a morning, boil once, make the first drink, and pour the rest into a vacuum flask. It stays hot for hours and you avoid reboiling from cold three or four times. One boil serves the morning.

Heat the water, not the air around it

One reason the kettle is the cheapest way to make a hot drink is that the element sits directly in the water. There is no hotplate to warm, no pan to heat, no flame heating the air. Nearly all the energy goes where you want it. That is also why the only real waste is in how much water you put in, not in the kettle's design.

It pays to think of the kettle alongside the other hot-water uses in the house, because the same rule runs through all of them: heat only what you need. A shower draws far more hot water than a kettle, so trimming shower time saves more than any kettle habit, and a low-flow showerhead cuts it further. If you heat water in a tank, lagging the cylinder and pipes stops the heat you paid for leaking away before you use it, as our cylinder jacket and pipe lagging guide covers. The kettle is the smallest of these, but it is the one you touch most often, so the habit sticks.

For context, the whole hot-water side of the bill, taps, showers and tank, is the second biggest energy use in a typical home after the heating itself. The wider hot water savings page sets out where it all goes.

Kettle versus hob versus microwave

People sometimes wonder whether boiling water another way is cheaper. For boiling water, the electric kettle is hard to beat.

A kettle puts nearly all its energy straight into the water, because the element sits in the water itself. Heating a mugful in a pan on an electric hob loses heat to the pan, the ring and the air around it, so it is slower and usually costs the same or more. A gas hob uses cheaper fuel per unit at 7.33p per kWh, so boiling a large pan of water for cooking can work out cheaper on gas, but for a single mug the kettle is faster and the difference is pennies either way. See induction versus gas hob for the wider hob comparison.

A microwave can heat a single mug of water for about the same cost as a kettle, but it is fiddly and uneven for more than a cup. For the everyday job of making tea or coffee, the kettle is the right tool. The waste is never the kettle itself, it is filling it to the brim for one cup.
