Hypermiling: cutting your fuel bill without becoming a hazard

Hypermiling is the art of driving to use as little fuel as possible. Taken sensibly it can cut a fuel bill by ten or fifteen per cent, which on a year's driving is real money. Taken to extremes it annoys other drivers, removes your safety margin, and is not worth the risk. The good news is that nearly all the saving comes from a handful of calm, undramatic habits that also make you a smoother and safer driver. This guide gives you the worthwhile middle: the techniques that genuinely work, the ones that do not, and the single rule that overrides the lot.

The short answer. The biggest fuel savings come from anticipation: reading the road far ahead so you lift off early and coast to a stop rather than accelerating up to it and braking. Accelerate gently, get into the highest sensible gear early, hold a steady speed, and ease off your top motorway speed because drag rises steeply with speed. Keep tyres correctly inflated, strip out dead weight, and remove roof bars when not in use. Done calmly this can cut fuel use by ten to fifteen per cent. Never let it make you a hazard.

The techniques that actually work

The single largest gain comes from anticipation. Reading the road far ahead lets you lift off the accelerator early and coast towards a red light, a queue or a roundabout, rather than accelerating up to it and braking hard at the last moment. Every time you brake, you are throwing away fuel you have just spent getting up to speed and converting it into heat in the brake discs. The smoother and earlier you read the road, the less of that you waste.

  • Anticipate and coast. Look well ahead, not just at the car in front. Lift off early for hazards and lights so momentum carries you, rather than power then brake.
  • Accelerate gently but purposefully. Crawling away from lights wastes fuel and holds up traffic; flooring it wastes more. A firm, smooth pull-away is the sweet spot.
  • Highest sensible gear, early. A low-revving engine in a high gear uses less fuel than a screaming one in a low gear. Change up sooner than instinct suggests, as long as the engine is not labouring.
  • Steady speed. Surging and slowing burns more than holding a constant pace. On a clear motorway, gentle use of cruise control helps.
  • Ease off the top end. Air resistance rises steeply with speed, so the last few miles per hour cost the most fuel. Dropping from 80 to 70, or 70 to 65, makes a measurable difference on a long run.

Keep the car helping you

Half of hypermiling happens before you turn the key. A car kept in good order uses less fuel for the same journey.

Tyres. Underinflated tyres roll harder, so the engine works more to overcome the drag. Check them at least monthly and before long trips, against the pressures in the door jamb or handbook. It is the cheapest fuel saving there is.

Weight. Every kilo you carry needs accelerating and lifting up hills. Clear the boot of the stuff that lives there for no reason. It is a small effect, but free.

Roof bars and boxes. The drag they add is far larger than people expect, especially on the motorway where air resistance dominates. Take them off when you are not using them; a roof box can knock a tenth off your economy at speed.

Servicing. A clogged air filter, the wrong oil, or a dragging brake quietly costs fuel. Keep up with the service schedule. If you also run an electric car, the same anticipation principles apply, covered in EV running cost versus petrol, and tyre and weight discipline help range too. In cold weather an EV loses range to heating and battery temperature, which the EV charging in winter guide covers.

Myths and things not worth it

Several popular hypermiling tricks save little, and some are dangerous. These are the ones to drop.

  • Coasting in neutral down hills. On a modern fuel-injected engine this saves almost nothing, because the engine already cuts fuel on the overrun when you lift off in gear. Worse, neutral removes engine braking and reduces your control, so it is a safety trade for no real gain.
  • Drafting behind lorries. Sitting in the slipstream of a truck does cut your drag, and it is genuinely dangerous, because you cannot see ahead and have no room to stop. Never do it.
  • Switching the engine off at every stop. Stop-start systems handle long waits automatically. Manually killing the engine at a brief junction halt saves nothing meaningful and risks restarting awkwardly when the queue moves. Save it for genuinely long waits like level crossings.
  • Over-inflating tyres beyond spec. Pumping tyres well above the recommended pressure to cut rolling resistance hardens the ride and reduces grip and braking. Stick to the handbook figure.

Air conditioning, heating and the little extras

The accessories you run while driving have a real but often overstated effect on fuel use, and the right call changes with the speed you are doing.

Air conditioning draws power from the engine, so it does cost a little fuel, but the effect is small at motorway speed and the popular alternative is worse. Driving fast with the windows down adds far more drag than the air conditioning costs, so at speed it is more economical to shut the windows and use the air con. In town at low speed the opposite holds: open a window and leave the air con off. The same trade-off matters for keeping a car comfortable in summer, covered in keeping your car cool.

Heated screens, seats and demisters draw current that the alternator has to replace by burning a little more fuel, so switch them off once they have done their job rather than leaving them on the whole journey. Heavy electrical loads in general, lights, blowers, charging several devices, add a small amount. Individually these are minor; the point is simply to turn off what you are not using rather than running everything out of habit. If you are weighing a car up against an electric one, the EV running cost versus petrol comparison is where the bigger numbers live, and the general fuel advice is on the fuel and driving page.

The one rule that overrides everything

Never let saving fuel turn you into a nuisance or a hazard. That single rule beats every technique above.

Crawling far below the flow of traffic, braking late so you can coast, hovering close to other vehicles, or hesitating at junctions to preserve momentum all undo the point. You frustrate other drivers, provoke risky overtakes, and put yourself in danger, and the fuel saved is not worth any of it.

The reassuring part is that proper hypermiling and proper driving are the same thing. Smooth, anticipatory, steady driving uses less fuel and is also simply safer and more relaxed. You are not adopting a strange new style; you are doing what a calm, observant driver does anyway. Keep it sensible, keep the car maintained, and the savings come without anyone else even noticing.
