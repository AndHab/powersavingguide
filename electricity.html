Electricity in the home: where it goes

Electricity bills feel mysterious, but the spending is not spread evenly across the house. A handful of things dominate, a few run quietly all year, and most gadgets are trivial. Knowing which is which tells you where effort is repaid and where it is wasted. This page is the overview. It maps where your electricity goes, then links to the deeper guides on appliances, standby, lighting and the running-cost calculator so you can work out your own numbers.

The short answer. Electricity spending is dominated by anything that makes heat: electric heating, the immersion heater, the tumble dryer, the oven, the kettle and the electric shower. After those come the fridge and freezer, modest moment to moment but never off. Everything else, the telly, the laptop, the lights, is usually small. Standby power is real but minor. Put your effort into the big heat-making users and the lighting swap to LED, and do not lose sleep over the gadgets.

The big users: anything that makes heat

The single most useful rule for electricity is that making heat is expensive. Turning electricity into warmth, whether for your body, your water, your food or your clothes, takes a lot of energy, and at 26.11p per kWh it shows up fast on the bill.

The heavy hitters are electric heating, the immersion heater, the tumble dryer, the oven, the kettle and the electric shower. These pull 2kW to 9kW while running. A 9kW electric shower for ten minutes uses 1.5 kWh, about 39p, every single day. A tumble dryer cycle can cost 50p to 80p. These are where the money is.

The fix is rarely to stop using them, but to use them less or smarter: shorter showers, drying clothes on a rack when you can (see drying clothes without a tumble dryer), and choosing the cheapest tool for the job, as in oven, microwave and air fryer compared.

The always-on crowd

After the big heat-makers come the appliances that never switch off. Individually they draw little, but they run 24 hours a day, every day, so they quietly add up over a year.

The fridge and freezer are the main ones. A modern efficient fridge-freezer might use 200 to 350 kWh a year, costing roughly £52 to £91, while an old or poorly placed one uses far more. Keeping it out of direct sun, away from the oven and with clean coils helps. The broadband router and similar small permanent loads are real but small.

Because these run constantly, efficiency matters more than for an occasional appliance. A unit that uses 50 kWh a year more than a better model costs an extra £13 every year for as long as you own it, which adds up over a decade. When a fridge or freezer is on its last legs, replacing it with a high-rated model can pay back through lower running costs, and our energy labels explained guide helps you read the rating. A few free habits help any fridge: keep the door seal clean and intact, let hot food cool before it goes in, and defrost a freezer when the ice builds up, because a thick layer of frost makes it work harder. The detail is in fridge and freezer efficiency.

Standby is real but small

Devices left on standby do draw power, and across a houseful of them over a year it adds up to a noticeable, if not enormous, sum, commonly £30 to £80. Older equipment and anything with a transformer that stays warm are the worst offenders. Switching things off at the wall is worth doing.

The worst standby offenders are usually the set-top box or games console that idles at 10 to 20W, an old plasma TV's standby circuit, and anything with a plug-in adaptor that feels warm to the touch, since that warmth is wasted electricity. A simple move is to put the TV and its peripherals on one switched extension lead and turn the lot off at bedtime.

But keep it in proportion. Standby is worth a tidy-up, not an obsession. The energy a tumble dryer or an electric shower uses in a week can exceed what every standby device in the house draws in a month. Sort the big users first, then deal with standby as a quick housekeeping job. The full picture, including which devices genuinely waste power on standby and which barely sip, is in standby power, the full story.

Lighting: the easy win

Lighting is not usually the biggest line on a bill, but it offers one of the cleanest savings going. If you still have halogen or old incandescent bulbs, swapping to LED is close to free money. An LED uses around a tenth to a fifth of the electricity for the same light and lasts for years, so the swap pays for itself quickly and then keeps paying with no further effort.

It is one of the few changes that is genuinely fit and forget. Replace the bulbs you use most first, the kitchen and living room, then swap the rest as they fail. Within a year the house is done.

The gap between the top and bottom rows is the whole point. The shower and dryer cost more in a fortnight than a TV's standby costs in a year. That is why effort spent on the big heat-makers pays, and effort spent chasing every standby light mostly does not.

Work out your own numbers

The figures above are typical, but your house is not typical, so put your own appliances through the running cost calculator. Seeing in black and white that the tumble dryer costs more in a year than the standby of every gadget combined is the moment the priorities click into place.

For the things you are unsure about, a plug-in energy monitor measures actual draw at the socket, which beats guessing from the label.
