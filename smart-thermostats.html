Smart thermostats: do they actually save money?

A smart thermostat lets you control your heating from your phone, learns your routine, and can hold back the heat when nobody is home. It is genuinely useful, and for the right household it pays for itself. But it is worth being clear-eyed about where the savings come from, because the device does not save energy by itself. It saves energy only by heating your home less and at better times, which is something you can do for nothing with the programmer you already have. This guide separates the real benefit from the marketing.

The short answer. A smart thermostat saves money by making it effortless to heat your home less and at the right times, not by any magic of its own. For a household with an irregular routine that often heats an empty house, the saving can be worth 50 to 150 a year, paying back the device in a couple of years. If you already run a tight, well-timed schedule by hand, it will save you very little.

What it actually does

At heart a smart thermostat does what a good programmable thermostat has always done: it matches heat to when you are in. The difference is that it makes that easy, and in places automatic, so it gets used properly rather than set once and forgotten.

The useful things it adds over an old dial-and-timer:

  • Phone control. Switch the heating off from the bus when you realise you left it on, or warm the house on your way home.
  • Presence detection. It can sense when the house is empty, through your phone's location, and hold back the heat without you remembering to.
  • Optimised start. It learns how long your home takes to warm up and fires the boiler at the right moment, so the house is warm when you want it rather than heating too early.
  • Clear scheduling. Setting a sensible weekly programme through an app is easier than wrestling with a clunky timer, so people actually do it.

The convenience is real, and it removes the friction that stops people bothering with timers at all. That friction is the whole reason most homes overheat empty rooms.

Where the saving really comes from

Here is the honest truth the adverts skate over. The saving comes from heating your home less, and at better times. Nothing else. A smart thermostat does not make your boiler more efficient or your gas cheaper. It just makes the good habits effortless.

You can get the same result for nothing with the programmer and thermostat you already have, plus a little discipline: set the heating to come on shortly before you get up and shortly before you get home, off when the house is empty and overnight, and the temperature no higher than you need. That is covered in full on the thermostat settings page and what temperature should I set my thermostat.

What the smart device adds is making that discipline automatic, so households that would never reliably fiddle with a timer end up heating an empty house far less. If you already run a tight, well-timed schedule by hand, and you turn the heating off when you go out, a smart model will save you almost nothing, because there is no waste left for it to catch. Be honest with yourself about which kind of household you are before you spend.

The device pays back fastest for the household that wastes the most heating today. If that is you, it is a sound buy and the convenience is a bonus. If you already heat carefully, buy it for the comfort and control if you want them, but do not expect it to cut the bill much. It will not run the boiler less than you already do.

Features worth having, and features that are marketing

If you do buy one, spend on the features that save money and ignore the ones that just look good in the app.

Worth having:

  • Presence detection or geofencing, so it holds back heat when everyone is out. This is where most of the saving comes from.
  • Individual room control through smart radiator valves, so you heat the rooms in use rather than the whole house. This is the zoning that genuinely cuts gas, the same idea as turning radiators down in unused rooms.
  • Optimised start, so the boiler fires at the right moment rather than too early.

Nice but secondary:

  • Learning algorithms that work out your routine for you. Helpful, but you can set a schedule yourself in five minutes.
  • Weather compensation, which trims the heat on milder days. A real but small effect, and your boiler flow temperature setting matters more.
  • Flashy app graphics and energy reports. Marketing. Zoning and occupancy sensing are where the money is saved, not the dashboard.

Fitting, compatibility and the running costs people forget

Before you buy, a few practical points decide whether a smart thermostat is straightforward or a faff in your home.

Compatibility. Most smart thermostats replace your existing wired thermostat or programmer and work with a standard gas combi or system boiler. Older systems, or some heat-only boilers, may need a bit more wiring. Check your boiler type against the model before buying, and budget for an electrician or heating engineer if you are not confident wiring it yourself. A combi boiler is usually the easy case.

The device draws power too. A smart thermostat and its hub sit on standby permanently, drawing a small amount of electricity year-round. It is only a pound or two a year, trivial against the heating it can save, but it is a fair reminder that the kit is not free to run, the same point made on the standby power page.

It needs your broadband. The phone control and presence detection rely on your home internet and the maker's servers. If your broadband drops, you lose the app control, though the thermostat keeps running its schedule on its own. That dependence is worth weighing if your connection is unreliable.

Who benefits most, and the free alternative

The best candidates for a smart thermostat are the households with the most wasted heating to catch:

  • People out at unpredictable hours, where a fixed timer never quite fits.
  • Homes that are often half empty during the day.
  • Households that have never reliably used the existing timer, so heat the place whether anyone is in or not.

The poorest fit is a household at home all day on a fixed, settled routine, like a retired couple who already heat sensibly. There is simply far less waste for the device to find, so the saving is small.

Before you spend, weigh the cost against your own pattern honestly, and remember the free version. Setting your existing programmer to match your actual routine, turning the thermostat down a degree or two, turning radiators down in rooms you do not use, and switching the heating off when you go out delivers much of the same benefit with no kit at all. Pair that with the cheap fabric jobs, draught-proofing and topping up loft insulation, and you cut the bill more than any thermostat can on its own. The smart device is worth buying when it makes those good habits stick, not as a substitute for them.
