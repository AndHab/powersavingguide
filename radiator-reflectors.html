Radiator reflectors: do they actually work?

A reflective panel slipped behind a radiator is one of those cheap measures that sounds like a gimmick but has a genuine, if modest, basis in physics. The idea is to bounce heat back into the room instead of letting it soak into the wall behind. On the right wall it does exactly that, and because it costs almost nothing, the sums work out in its favour. On the wrong wall it does nothing at all. This guide explains the principle, which radiators are worth treating, how to fit a reflector for a few pounds, and why you should keep your expectations realistic.

The short answer. Radiator reflectors work, but only behind radiators on external walls, and only modestly. They reflect radiant heat back into the room instead of letting it warm the outside wall and escape. Expect a saving of a few pounds per radiator per year, best in older solid-wall homes with poor insulation. They cost almost nothing, so the payback is quick, but treat them as a finishing touch after the bigger fabric measures, not a headline saving.

The principle: where the heat goes

A radiator does two jobs. It warms the air that drifts past it, and it radiates heat directly outward, including straight into the wall it is mounted on. That wall warms up, and where the heat goes next depends on what is on the other side.

If the wall is internal, the heat simply passes into the next room of your own house, which is no loss. If the wall is external, with cold outdoors on the far side, much of that heat conducts straight through the bricks and escapes. A reflective panel sits between the radiator and the wall and bounces the radiant heat back into the room rather than letting it warm the masonry and disappear.

The effect is small per radiator, because most of a radiator's output goes into the air, not into the wall behind it. But the cost is tiny, so even a small percentage saving repays a few pounds of foil quickly. It belongs in the same category as draught-proofing and heavier curtains: cheap, undramatic, worth doing.

Only behind external walls

This is the part people miss, and getting it wrong means wasted effort. A reflector only helps behind a radiator on an external wall, the kind with cold outdoors on the far side. A radiator on an internal wall is warming another part of your own home through that wall, so reflecting the heat back saves nothing worth bothering with.

Walk around the house and work out which radiators sit against outside walls. The easy tell is to go outside and see which walls face the open air, then map them to the radiators inside. Radiators under windows are almost always on external walls, because that is where the cold draught comes in, so those are usually worth treating. Radiators on a party wall with a neighbour, or on a wall dividing two of your own rooms, are not.

Treat only the external-wall radiators. Reflectors behind internal ones are money and effort spent for no return.

Fitting one cheaply

There are two routes, and neither needs tools beyond scissors or a craft knife.

Buy a purpose-made panel. These come with a foil face and a thin insulating backing, sometimes with tabs or brackets to hang them on the radiator wall-brackets so they tuck neatly behind. They are inexpensive and quick to fit. Slide the panel down behind the radiator so the foil faces into the room.

Make your own. Cut rigid foil-faced insulation board (the kind sold for general insulation jobs) to a size that fits the gap, and slide it down behind the radiator. At the cheapest end, good-quality kitchen foil stuck shiny-side-out onto stiff card does much the same job, though it is flimsier and harder to position. The foil-faced board is far better because the insulating layer behind the foil adds a little to the effect.

Whichever route you take, it tucks out of sight, you fit it once, and then you forget about it. There is no maintenance and nothing to go wrong.

How much it actually saves

Be realistic. The honest figure for a reflector is small, in the low single pounds per radiator per year, and it is bigger on poorly insulated walls than well insulated ones. Here is a rough sense of scale across a house.

Across a few external-wall radiators in an older home you might save ten or fifteen pounds a year for a one-off outlay of a few pounds. That is a good return in percentage terms, but a small one in absolute money. Keep it in proportion.

Getting the most from the heat you do reflect

A reflector only helps if the heat it bounces back actually reaches the room rather than getting trapped behind an obstruction. A few habits make the modest saving go a little further.

  • Keep the radiator clear. A sofa pushed against a radiator, or a long curtain hanging in front of it, soaks up the very heat you are trying to send into the room. The reflector pushes heat forward; do not then block it. Tuck curtains behind the radiator or onto the windowsill so warm air rises into the room. The case for curtains for warmth is about the window, not draping over the radiator.
  • Bleed the radiator. A radiator with trapped air is cold at the top and gives out less heat, so there is less for the reflector to work with. Bleeding it takes a couple of minutes and restores full output.
  • Mind the gap. The reflector sits in the gap between radiator and wall, so it should not touch the radiator or block airflow up the back of it. A neat fit that leaves the convection path clear works best, and a panel cut slightly oversized can be trimmed to suit.

None of this transforms the saving, but it makes sure the small gain a reflector offers is not quietly cancelled out by a curtain or a sofa in the way.

Where it sits in the bigger picture

Reflectors are a genuine but minor saving, firmly in the every-little-helps bracket rather than the big wins. They matter most in older, poorly insulated solid-wall homes where the wall behind the radiator is doing little to hold heat in. If your walls are already well insulated, there is very little for a reflector to claw back, because the wall is already keeping the heat inside.

So spend your effort on the bigger fabric measures first. Loft insulation, cavity wall insulation or solid wall insulation, and draught-proofing dwarf what any reflector can do. Getting your thermostat right and using radiator valves to zone your heating save far more for no cost at all. Treat reflectors as a cheap finishing touch once the larger jobs are done, not as a substitute for them.

One honest caveat on the marketing. Some sellers quote eye-catching savings per radiator, drawn from tests on uninsulated solid walls under ideal conditions. Real homes rarely hit those numbers, and a house that has already had cavity or solid wall insulation will see almost nothing. So buy the cheap foil-faced board, fit it behind your external-wall radiators in an afternoon, and be pleased with a small saving rather than expecting the figures on the packet.
